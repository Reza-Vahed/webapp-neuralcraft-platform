"We need an AI strategy" has become almost as common a sentence as "we should move to the cloud." Well-intentioned, but rarely followed by a clear next step. After running strategy projects with numerous small and medium-sized businesses, a simple pattern has proven itself.

## Step 1: Processes before technology

Before talking about models, vendors, or tools, one question needs an answer: **where are we demonstrably losing time or money today to manual, repetitive work?** This isn't an academic exercise — usually half a day with the right people in the room is enough to identify three to five concrete candidates.

## Step 2: Prioritize by effort and impact

Not every process is equally suited. We evaluate candidates along two axes:

1. **Impact**: how much time, money, or error rate is at stake?
2. **Implementation effort**: how clean is the data, how complex is the process really?

The best first use case is rarely the one with the biggest impact — it's the one with the best ratio of impact to feasibility. A fast, visible win builds the trust needed for the next, bigger steps.

## Step 3: A roadmap, not a study

The outcome shouldn't be a 40-page PDF that ends up in a drawer, but a roadmap with:

- concrete use cases in implementation order,
- a realistic effort estimate per step,
- and a success metric defined _before_ the work starts.

## Step 4: Implement, measure, keep going

The most important difference between an AI strategy that actually moves the needle and one that disappears into a drawer: after the first use case, the next step isn't another concept paper — it's the next implementation. For us, strategy and execution are deliberately not separate projects. That's precisely the point.
